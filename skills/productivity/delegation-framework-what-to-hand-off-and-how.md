A decision matrix for deciding what to delegate, plus a handoff template that actually transfers context.

## When to use
Whenever your task list is too large for you alone, or when you want to develop team members.

## Delegation decision matrix
```
Ask two questions:
1. Does this require MY unique knowledge/authority? 
2. Is this a growth opportunity for a team member?

         | Q2: Yes          | Q2: No           |
---------|------------------|------------------|
Q1: Yes  | Do + debrief     | Do               |
Q1: No   | DELEGATE (grow)  | DELEGATE (free)  |
```

If Q1 = No, the task should almost always be delegated.

## Delegation levels
- **Level 1** — Do exactly this: [instructions]
- **Level 2** — Figure out options, come back with a recommendation
- **Level 3** — Make the decision and let me know what you did
- **Level 4** — Own this fully; escalate only if [specific condition]

State the level explicitly. Most delegation failures are Level 4 intent communicated as Level 1.

## Handoff template
```markdown
### Delegating: [Task name]
**To**: [Name]
**Level**: [1/2/3/4 — see above]
**Due**: [Date]

**Context**: [Why this exists, what outcome we want]
**Constraints**: [Non-negotiables — budget, tools, stakeholders]
**Resources**: [Links, contacts, prior work]
**Definition of done**: [How will we both know it's complete?]
**Check-in**: [When will we sync? Or "ping me if blocked"]
```

## Tips
- "Can you take a look at this?" is not a delegation. Specify Level, Due, and Definition of Done.
- Level 3–4 delegation requires trust built over time. Start at Level 2 with new team members.
- After handoff, resist taking the task back. If the person is stuck, coach — don't solve.
